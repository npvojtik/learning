Grammar

Noun Classification System (Gender)

German has three words for 'the': der, das, and die, and German nouns can be divided into three classes according to which of the three words for 'the'they go with.

There are no general rules for classification of the majority of German nouns. For derivative nouns, there are some rules:

  • Nouns ending in -ung always take die, e.g. die Verzeihung (the forgiveness, the pardon)
  • Nouns ending with the diminutive suffixes -lein and -chen always take das, e.g. das Fräulein (the miss, waitress), das Mädchen (the little girl)

Pronouns Corresponding to the Three Classes of Nouns

There is a special pronoun for each of the three classes of noun: er for der, es for das, and sie for die:

Wo ist der Bahnhof? – Er ist dort.Where is the station? – It's there.
Wo ist der Mann? – Er ist dort.Where is the man? – He's there.
Wo ist das Hotel? – Es ist dort.Where is the hotel? – It's there.
Wo ist das Kind? – Es ist dort.Where is the child? – (S)he's there.
Wo ist die Botschaft? – Sie ist dort.Where is the embassy? – It's there.
Wo ist die Frau? – Sie ist dort.Where is the woman? – She's there.

As in these examples, er and sie correspond to 'he' and 'she' when referring to human beings. So you say sie when referring to das Fräulein and to die Tochter.

No Classification in the Plural

Gender only matters in the singular. In the plural, all forms take die, and all use the pronoun sie:

Wo sind die Bahnhöfe? – Sie sind hier.Where are the stations? – They're here.
Wo sind die Hotels? – Sie sind hier.Where are the hotels? – They're here.
Wo sind die Frauen? – Sie sind hier.Where are the women? – They're here.

Pointing Word das

The pointing word das is used to call any object or group of objects to the hearer's attention, without any reference to noun classification or to the number of objects. This is an entirely different entity from the das of das Hotel. The English equivalent would be 'this', 'that', 'these', and 'those' (in the nominal form, not the determinative form).

Das ist der Bahnhof, nicht wahr?
This is the station, isn't it?
Ja, das ist er.
Yes, that's it.

Ist das die Milch?
Is that the milk?
Das ist Wasser.
This is water.

Sind das die Streichhölzer?
Are these the matches?
Das sind die Zigarren.
Those are the cigars.

Grammar

Pronoun Form and Function

Pronouns in German have multiple forms with different functions. Most have three (e.g. ich, mich, mir), but some have two (e.g. Sie, Ihnen).

Nominative

In German, the basic sentence structure is an actor-action pattern: somebody doing something. The actor is the subject of the sentence, and in German a pronoun designating the actor always has the nominative form.

  • Ich verstehe sehr gut.
    I understand very well.
  • Er wohnt ganz in meiner Nähe.
    He lives quite near me.
  • Wir wollen gerade essen gehen.
    We're just planning to go and eat.

Accusative

In many sentences there is a goal or object of an action, the person or thing toward which the action is aimed. In German a pronoun designating the object of an action is usually in the accusative form.

  • Ich kenne ihn sehr gut.
    I know him very well.
  • Verstehen Sie mich?
    Do you understand me?
  • Wir treffen sie dort.
    We're meeting them (or her) there.

Dative

The third form, the dative, designates an interested bystander, the person to whom or for whom or with regard to whom the action of the sentence is being performed. Sometimes this called the indirect object.

  • Können Sie mir seine Adresse geben?
    Can you give me his address?
  • Sie müssen ihm Ihren Pass zeigen.
    You have to show your passport to him.
  • Wie geht es Ihnen?
    How are you? (How goes it with regards to you?)
  • Gefällt es Ihnen in München?
    Do you like it in Munich? (Is it pleasing to you in Munich?)
  • Der Koffer gehört mir nicht.
    The suitcase doesn't belong to me.

The dative is also requried by some prepositions. For example, any pronoun that follows mit must use the dative:

  • Sprechen Sie englisch mit ihr?
    Do you speak English with her?
  • Ich fahre oft mit ihm nach Hause.
    I often ride home with him.

Principles of Substitution

When a certain form is required, any substitutions must also be in that form. For example, the mich in Er kennt mich is in accusative. You can replace it with uns, es, sie wen (in a question), and any other pronoun, but that pronoun must be in the accusative form.

Similarly, dative forms must be replaced by other dative forms and nominative forms by other nominative forms.

Present Tense Verb Function

German does not have an -ing form (like English does), so the simple present is used to accomplish the same function:

  • er gibt = he gives, he's giving, he does give
  • gibt er? = is he giving?, does he give?

For commands, German uses the verb form with the ending -en, followed by the pronoun Sie:

  • Bitte sprechen Sie langsam.
    Please speak slowly.
  • Zeigen Sie mir bitte Ihr Gepäck.
    Show me your luggage please.

Two verb forms can be used together in German or in English as a verb phrase. Although German doesn have verb phrases like "is going" or "does believe", it has others:

  • Ich möchte gern Wasser haben.
    I'd like to have water.
  • Können Sie mich gut verstehen?
    Can you understand me well?
  • Darf ich Ihren Pass sehen?
    May I see your passport?
  • Sie müssen zum Zoll gehen.
    You have to go to the customs office.

The second part of the German verb phrase comes at the end of the sentence and is called the infinitive. It is the form ending in -en but it never has a subejct and is unaffected by any change of subject (e.g. Ich kann gehen and Wir können gehen). The only irregular infinitive in German is sein, ('to be') – for every other verb, the infinitive is just the same as the wir form.

Hin and Her

Wo by itself means 'where' in the sense of 'in what place?'. Her adds the meaning "from" and hin adds the meaning "to":

  • Wo wohnen Sie?
    Where do you live?
  • Wo kommen Sie her?
    Where do you come from?
  • Wo gehen Sie hin?
    Where are you going (to)?

The position of hin and her is usually at the end of the sentence or question, but they may also occur immediately after (and joined to) wo (or other adverb):

  • Wo fahren Sie heute hin?
    Where are you going today?
  • Wohin fahren Sie heute?
    Where are you going today?
  • Wir fahren dorthin.
    We are going (to) there.

MasculineFeminine

Spanish nouns have a set gender and inflect for number. Generally, el and la are the masculine and feminine definite articles. El can also appear before feminine words that begin with a or h for pronunciation reasons (e.g. el agua and el hacha are both feminine).

Articles and adjectives must inflect for gender and number. Note: not all adjectives inflect for gender. For example, mucho becomes mucha in front of a feminine noun, but menos stays the same for both masculine and feminine nouns.

Usage Notes

  1. Spanish does not have a literal way to say “I like [it]”; instead, Spanish uses a construction that literally means “[it] pleases me.”
  2. Agua mineral may be mineral spring water, sparkling water, or purified drinking water. When one company dominates the purified water market in a country, its name becomes generic, as with “Band-aid” in America.
  3. Cerca (“near”) modifies a verb but does not function as a phrase relator. In English, we can say “He gets near” and “he gets near the house” – in Spanish, you have to use cerca de for the second meaning.
  4. Hay que literally means “it is necessary to (...)”, but it is commonly used to mean “you’ve got to (...)”, “you have to (...)” with an impersonal “you” (like “one should” or “one ought to”).
  5. In Mexico, the first floor is often called planta baja and elevator buttons are marked PB.

SingularPlural
1Psoysomos

Ser is the link of identity (the essential, inherent, or permanent) while estar is the link of association (the causal, accidental, or temporary). For example, "Juan es mi hermano" but "Juan está en el pasillo."

With adjectives, the difference usually expresses a degree, intensity, or sentiment of intrinsicness. For example, "La casa es bonita" expresses that the house is intrinsically pretty, while "La casa está bonita" means that the house is unusually attractive on a specific occasion, e.g. it looks nice(r than usual) today.

There are also some set usages for each verb. For example, the location of people or places is always expressed with estar, regardless of how permanent or impermanent. A few more examples:

  • ¿Cómo está María? – How is Maria?
    ¿Cómo es María? – What is Maria like?
  • ¿Dónde está su marido? – Where is your husband?
    ¿De dónde es su marido? – Where is your husband from?

Usage Notes

  1. When talking about marital status, some places use ser and some places use estar.

Correct stress placement is incredibly important in conjugation, as verbs may have multiple forms with the same spelling but only the stress differentiates them (e.g. compro vs. compró, two different subjects and two different tenses).

Estar vs. Haber

When a reference to the location of something is definite, estar is used, with the definiteness marked by the occurrence of a definite article. When the reference is not definite, haber is used, usually (but not necessarily) accompanied by an indefinite article. For example:

  • ¿Dónde está la silla? – Where is the chair?
    ¿Dónde hay una silla? – Where is there a chair?
  • Los libros están en la mesa. – The books are on the table.
    Hay (unos) libros en la mesa. – There are (some) books on the table.

Even though a definite number may be used, the reference may still be linguistically indefinite:

  • Hay dos plumas aquí. – There are two pens here.
  • Las dos plumas están aquí. – The two pens are here.

Usage Notes

  1. Vino is also often used to mean alcoholic beverages in general, not just wine. E.g. "El toma mucho vino" = "he drinks a lot."
  2. Even though clitic pronouns can already express ideas like "to me", you can also add "a mí" for emphasis or to express contrast. For example, "a mí tráigame sopa" means "bring me soup (not salad, like she's having)."
  3. Buscar means "to look for" – you don't need to say "buscar para", as the meaning conveyed by the preposition in English is already wrapped into the Spanish verb.
  4. Mozo is sometimes used by Latin Americans for "waiter"; it also means "boy", but it can be very condescending, especially to older waiters or waiters in formal establishments. Use mesero instead.

Obligatory Contractions

Spanish is full of telescoping forms, especially when two vowels come together between words. The contraction is usually complete if the two vowels are the same. For example, the final and initial "e"s of dónde está are run together into one, dóndestá. For emphasis, or for clarity, the distinction of the two words may be retained.

Some contractions are obligatory, even when slowed down or emphasized. The two most common are "de el" becoming "del" and "a el" becoming "al". These contractions are observed even in writing.

Note that the strong-stressed él (he) does not combine. So "his car", el coche de él, keeps the two words separate in both speech and writing.

Usage Notes

  1. Caramba is a euphemism for carajo, which is a pretty strong cuss-word in Spanish.
  2. There is some interchangeability between mudar and cambiar. Una muda de ropa and un cambio de ropa both mean "a change of clothes", and you can say "I'm moving" via both me cambio de casa and me mudo de casa. In general, use mudar to mean "move" and cambiar to mean "change"

Haber also means "to have", but in a different way than tener, which expresses possession. Haber is used with the past participle of a verb to form the present perfect ("I have ...ed"), e.g. yo he terminado.

Possessives have a number of reference (the selection of form, based on the subject, e.g. mío versus nuestro) and a number of agreement (the selection number, ased on agreement with the referent noun, e.g. mío versus míos).

  5. Cuba libre is rum and [coca] cola, usually with a slice of limón (in this case, lime).
  6. Lo means something like "the matter, the business, the idea previously mentioned, about ..." and is often used in nominalization, e.g. lo del apartamento tuyo = "about your apartment."
