Haber also means "to have", but in a different way than tener, which expresses possession. Haber is used with the past participle of a verb to form the present perfect ("I have ...ed"), e.g. yo he terminado.

The present perfect is used in Spanish much like in English, although it is less frequently used in Spanish.

Note that there is another singular variation of haber, hay ("there is", "there are") that is never used with a participle and does not inflect for subject number.

Possessives have a number of reference (the selection of form, based on the subject, e.g. mío versus nuestro) and a number of agreement (the selection number, ased on agreement with the referent noun, e.g. mío versus míos).

Possessives usually appear in their full form immediately after the noun they modify (la casa suya) or after the verb ser (es tuyo). Following a noun, these forms are similar to and translated by an English construction consisting of a noun plus "of" plus the long form of the possessive. E.g. un libro mío = a book of mine.

Usage Notes

  1. The diminutives -ito/a and -illo/a are used quite often in Spanish, especially in Mexico. They can indicate affection (mamita = mommy dear), sympathy (pobrecito = poor thing), and even patronizing contempt (morenito = a racial epithet in Western South America). It can also be used for emphasis, as is meant by igualito. There is also an -ico/a form that is often used in Costa Rica.
  2. Expressions of possession are common in Spanish, e.g. estás in tu casa means "make yourself at home", and if you express admiration for someone's car they may tell you que es tuyo.
  3. Many Spanish words for two foods in combination use con where English would use "and": whiskey con soda, tocino con huevos, arroz con pollo.
  4. Novio/a can mean anything from "the person I'm currently going out with" to "my fiancé(e)". The bride and groom at a wedding are also called novio and novia. The meaning must be judged from context.
  5. Cuba libre is rum and [coca] cola, usually with a slice of limón (in this case, lime).
  6. Lo means something like "the matter, the business, the idea previously mentioned, about ..." and is often used in nominalization, e.g. lo del apartamento tuyo = "about your apartment."
