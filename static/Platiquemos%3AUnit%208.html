Obligatory Contractions

Spanish is full of telescoping forms, especially when two vowels come together between words. The contraction is usually complete if the two vowels are the same. For example, the final and initial "e"s of dónde está are run together into one, dóndestá. For emphasis, or for clarity, the distinction of the two words may be retained.

Some contractions are obligatory, even when slowed down or emphasized. The two most common are "de el" becoming "del" and "a el" becoming "al". These contractions are observed even in writing.

Note that the strong-stressed él (he) does not combine. So "his car", el coche de él, keeps the two words separate in both speech and writing.

Usage Notes

  1. Caramba is a euphemism for carajo, which is a pretty strong cuss-word in Spanish.
  2. There is some interchangeability between mudar and cambiar. Una muda de ropa and un cambio de ropa both mean "a change of clothes", and you can say "I'm moving" via both me cambio de casa and me mudo de casa. In general, use mudar to mean "move" and cambiar to mean "change"
