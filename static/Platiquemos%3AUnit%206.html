Correct stress placement is incredibly important in conjugation, as verbs may have multiple forms with the same spelling but only the stress differentiates them (e.g. compro vs. compró, two different subjects and two different tenses).

Estar vs. Haber

When a reference to the location of something is definite, estar is used, with the definiteness marked by the occurrence of a definite article. When the reference is not definite, haber is used, usually (but not necessarily) accompanied by an indefinite article. For example:

  • ¿Dónde está la silla? – Where is the chair?
    ¿Dónde hay una silla? – Where is there a chair?
  • Los libros están en la mesa. – The books are on the table.
    Hay (unos) libros en la mesa. – There are (some) books on the table.

Even though a definite number may be used, the reference may still be linguistically indefinite:

  • Hay dos plumas aquí. – There are two pens here.
  • Las dos plumas están aquí. – The two pens are here.

Usage Notes

  1. Vino is also often used to mean alcoholic beverages in general, not just wine. E.g. "El toma mucho vino" = "he drinks a lot."
  2. Even though clitic pronouns can already express ideas like "to me", you can also add "a mí" for emphasis or to express contrast. For example, "a mí tráigame sopa" means "bring me soup (not salad, like she's having)."
  3. Buscar means "to look for" – you don't need to say "buscar para", as the meaning conveyed by the preposition in English is already wrapped into the Spanish verb.
  4. Mozo is sometimes used by Latin Americans for "waiter"; it also means "boy", but it can be very condescending, especially to older waiters or waiters in formal establishments. Use mesero instead.
